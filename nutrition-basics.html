Daily training

Consistency beats perfection: enough calories and carbs to support your load, protein spread through the day, and vegetables or fruit when you can.

  • Energy availability. Chronic underfueling relative to training load is linked to health and performance harm (including low energy availability / REDs risk). Skipping fuel to “save calories” often backfires as fatigue, poor sleep, or lost training quality.
  • Protein day-total. When energy needs are met, many training athletes land near ~1.2–2.0 g protein per kg body weight per day from food (not a rule—appetite, GI tolerance, and medical advice come first).
  • Easy days. Normal balanced meals; you don’t need race-level carbs every day unless volume is very high.
  • Hard days. Emphasize carbs before and after; add salt or a full meal if the session was long and sweaty.
  • Iron & vitamin D. Common discussion topics with doctors—especially if you’re tired, vegetarian, or train indoors; labs beat guessing.

Long runs

Long runs are dress rehearsal for fueling, not just mileage. GI comfort at race pace matters more than what works on the couch.

  • Carbohydrate per hour. Endurance fueling is usually framed by duration, not miles: many consensus-style summaries cite about 30–60 g/h for roughly 1–3 h of work, and higher intakes for long ultras with gut training. Glucose + fructose mixes help push absorption past ~60 g/h. Start low if you’re new; increase only if your stomach agrees.
  • Fluids. Practice carrying bottles or using loops where you can drink on schedule; note what you need in heat vs. cool weather.
  • Whole foods. Potatoes, dates, or bars work for some people; they take more chewing and water—test them before committing on race day.
  • Caffeine. If you use it, practice the dose and timing; cutting it on race day can cause headaches if you’re a daily drinker.

Hydration & electrolytes

Hydration is individual: sweat rate, saltiness of sweat, weather, and pace all change the answer.

  • Thirst & schedule. In mild conditions, drinking to thirst often works. In heat or humidity, a timed sip pattern (e.g., every 15–20 minutes) can prevent falling behind.
  • Sodium. Long or salty-sweat sessions may need sports drinks, electrolyte mixes, or small amounts of salt—especially if you cramp or feel dizzy.
  • Overdrinking. Chugging plain water for hours without sodium can be risky; use electrolytes for long efforts and don’t force fluid past comfort.
  • Sweat test (informal). Weighing before and after a hard hour in similar conditions gives a rough idea of fluid loss; adjust next time.
  • Before long sessions. Aim to start in a normal hydrated state; many fluid guidelines use ~5–7 mL/kg fluid in the ~4 hours before exercise as a planning anchor (add sodium-containing drink or food if that matches your plan).

Ultras & race day

Ultras reward patience: steady intake, flexible plans, and aid-station choices that still sound good hours in.

  • Variety. Sweet, salty, and savory options help when one flavor stops appealing. Know what’s at your race or carry backups.
  • Small & often. Frequent modest calories beat rare huge feeds that can sit in your stomach.
  • Night & cold. Appetite often drops; warm broth, noodles, or handheld foods can be easier than cold gels.
  • Crew & drop bags. Label bottles and foods; write a simple hourly target (fluid + carbs) your crew can help you hit.
  • Sodium with fluid on long days. Field and sports-medicine guidance often pairs carbohydrate + sodium + fluid for multi-hour efforts to support retention and palatability—tune to your sweat, not generic social-media doses.

When things go wrong

Common issues—always rule out medical causes with a professional if symptoms are new or severe.

Bonking (sudden energy crash)

Often linked to low muscle glycogen or underfueling during the run. Next time: start carbs earlier, increase hourly intake gradually, and ensure recent meals supported the workout.

Nausea or sloshing stomach

Try smaller sips, fewer osmotically dense drinks at once, cooler fluids, or slowing pace briefly. Sometimes less fiber and fat pre-run helps.

Cramping

Not always “electrolytes,” but sodium and fluid mismatch is one piece. Fitness, heat, and pacing matter too—note patterns across runs.

No appetite late in long races

Liquids and semi-solids (broth, smoothies, mashed foods) can keep calories coming. Cool flavors and salty options often work when sweet does not.
